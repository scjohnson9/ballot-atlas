## What the Climate Commitment Act Does

Washington's Climate Commitment Act, enacted in 2021, establishes a cap-and-invest program: the state sets a declining cap on greenhouse-gas emissions from major emitters (fuel suppliers, utilities, large industrial sources), and those emitters must purchase allowances at quarterly auctions to cover their emissions. The cap declines over time to drive emissions toward Washington's statutory targets.

The auction proceeds — roughly $2 billion in the program's first eighteen months — fund state spending on transit, electric-vehicle infrastructure, clean-energy projects, building electrification, and aid to communities most affected by climate change.

## What I-2117 Does

I-2117 would repeal the Climate Commitment Act in its entirety. It would also prohibit the state legislature or any state agency from implementing any future cap-and-trade or cap-and-invest carbon-pricing program. The state could still pursue environmental policy through other means — direct regulation, tax incentives, public investment — but the specific cap-and-invest mechanism would be foreclosed.

## Two Christian Considerations

**Stewardship.** The doctrine of creation grounds a deep Christian commitment to the care of the natural world. Genesis 2 places the human creature in the garden "to till it and keep it" — a charge of cultivation, not consumption. The patristic tradition (Basil, Chrysostom), the medieval (Bonaventure, Aquinas), and the modern (Schaeffer, John Paul II, Bartholomew I) have all affirmed that creation is gift and trust. Carbon emissions that destabilize the climate system are, on this view, a matter of moral concern, and policies that price emissions are one available means of expressing that concern in law.

**Charity toward the poor.** The same tradition has been unsparing in its attention to the costs that fall on those least able to bear them. Carbon pricing is necessarily regressive in its direct incidence: the poor spend a higher fraction of their income on fuel and heat than the wealthy do. Policies that aim at long-term planetary good while imposing short-term costs on the economically vulnerable demand careful weighing — particularly when the alternative is not "no climate policy" but "climate policy by a different mechanism."

## The Hard Question

Neither side of I-2117 represents an obvious Christian answer. A vote to repeal is not a vote against creation-care; it is a vote against this particular mechanism for pursuing it, in favor of (potentially) different mechanisms. A vote to retain is not a vote against the economically vulnerable; it is a vote that the long-term stewardship gain is worth the short-term distributional cost.

CCTE commends I-2117 to Washington voters as a question of genuine prudential weight, and encourages voters to evaluate both the specific design of the Act (how the proceeds are spent, whether the offsets to low-income households are adequate) and the broader question of which policy mechanisms best honor both creation-care and charity.
