## The Two Changes Amendment 79 Makes

Amendment 79 is sometimes described in shorthand as a measure to "protect the right to abortion in Colorado." The shorthand is misleading. Colorado has no statutory restrictions on abortion to protect — the state permits the procedure at any gestational age, for any reason. Amendment 79 does not change what is legal.

It changes two other things.

First, it writes a right to abortion into the state constitution. This elevates the existing statutory permission to constitutional status, placing it beyond the reach of the legislature should Colorado voters one day elect representatives who would regulate the procedure.

Second, and more concretely, it repeals the 1984 constitutional prohibition on the public funding of abortion. Under current law, Colorado's Medicaid program and state-employee insurance plans cannot cover elective abortion procedures. Amendment 79 would remove that prohibition.

## Why the Funding Question Matters

For four decades, Colorado has lived under a bipartisan compromise: legal access to abortion, but no taxpayer obligation to pay for it. That compromise has held through every governor and legislature since 1984, across changes in party control, across the *Casey* decision and the *Dobbs* decision and every cultural shift in between.

The compromise is significant because conscience matters. A substantial share of Coloradans — Catholics, evangelical Protestants, Orthodox Christians, observant Jews and Muslims, and many secular citizens — hold the view that abortion ends a human life. To require those citizens to fund procedures to which they have a settled conscientious objection is to impose a moral burden on them that the state has, until now, declined to impose.

## A Note for Reflection

Christians may differ on the prudence of specific abortion laws. They may differ on what restrictions are achievable or wise in a state as legally permissive as Colorado. The question Amendment 79 puts to voters is not those questions but a different one: whether to remove a forty-year conscience protection and write into the state's basic charter a right whose moral premise — that the unborn child is not a person to whom the law owes any duty — the historic Christian tradition has consistently denied.

CCTE encourages Colorado voters to reject Amendment 79.
