## The Question Before Voters

Florida Amendment 3 would amend the state constitution to legalize the recreational use, possession, and purchase of marijuana for adults over 21. Like Amendment 4, it requires 60% voter approval to pass.

The amendment authorizes the state's existing medical-marijuana dispensaries to sell to recreational customers and grants the legislature authority to license additional retailers. Home cultivation by individuals is not legalized.

## The Christian Tradition on Substances and Prudence

Scripture is unsparing on drunkenness — the Old Testament wisdom literature, the Pauline epistles, and the patristic tradition all treat intoxication as a serious moral failure. But none of these texts specifies a particular civil regime for the regulation of intoxicants. The Christian tradition has, accordingly, lived alongside many different legal frameworks: wine consumed at every common meal in ancient Mediterranean Christianity; legal prohibition in early-twentieth-century America; decriminalization in much of Europe today.

The question Amendment 3 raises is not, *"Is it morally good to use marijuana?"* — to which the tradition would answer with the same caution it brings to all intoxicants — but rather, *"What civil regime best promotes the common good?"* That is a question of prudence.

## Voices on Both Sides

Christians who favor legalization point to the harms of criminal enforcement (disproportionately borne by communities of color), to the principle of subsidiarity (the state ought not to criminalize what is properly addressed by personal and familial formation), and to the inconsistency of allowing alcohol and tobacco while criminalizing a substance with comparable or lesser harm profiles.

Christians who oppose legalization point to the documented increase in use and addiction in states that have legalized; the rise in high-potency products that did not exist in earlier generations; the demonstrated effects on adolescent brain development; and the principle that the state has a positive role in shaping a culture of virtue, not merely in restraining manifest harms.

Both arguments are made in good faith from within the tradition.

## A Note on Discernment

CCTE issues no recommendation on Amendment 3 because we do not believe the historic Christian moral tradition settles the question in either direction. We commend the measure to Florida voters as a question warranting careful, prayerful reflection on the kind of civic life their state should pursue — and on the role of law in shaping it.
